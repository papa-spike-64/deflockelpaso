Why This Matters

Three things El Paso City Council should know before renewing

01 / SECURITY

22 Confirmed Vulnerabilities

The National Vulnerability Database lists 22 confirmed security flaws in Flock devices. Cameras can be physically hacked in 30 seconds. At one point, Flock admin dashboards were publicly accessible without a password.

02 / DATA ACCESS

Who Is Accessing El Paso's Data?

El Paso PD's own transparency portal lists a Harris County commissioner's office — not a law enforcement agency — as having access to El Paso camera data. No explanation has been provided to residents.

03 / CONTRACT CHANGES

Flock Quietly Rewrote Its Terms

In February 2026, Flock rewrote its Terms of Service with 147 changes — including deleting the clause that said "Flock does not own and shall not sell Customer Data." El Paso residents should know what terms a renewal would be signed under.

El Paso Specific

What makes El Paso's situation unique

Texas has no ALPR protection law

Texas has no state law governing how license plate reader data must be stored, who can access it, or how long it can be kept. Multiple bills have been introduced in Austin — all died in committee. El Paso residents have no state-level legal backstop. Governance depends entirely on the contract and the vendor's honesty.

Texas DPS Investigation

The Texas Department of Public Safety launched an investigation into Flock Safety over the company's private security license — which is required to legally operate in Texas. The investigation's outcome directly affects whether El Paso's contract is even on solid legal ground.

City Rep. Lily Limón's own words, March 3, 2026

"If you really read the Flock contract, data will not be shared unless required by law. Can we agree on this? That's where the back door can be opened." — Rep. Limón voted to end the contract. She deserves community support.

"We're not opposed to law enforcement having modern tools. We're asking whether this specific vendor — with this track record — can be trusted with El Paso's data."

Common Questions

Frequently asked

Are you trying to defund the police?

No. We support El Paso PD having effective tools. We are asking whether this specific vendor — one with documented security failures, a quietly rewritten contract, and a history of enabling unauthorized data access — is the right choice for El Paso. Good policing deserves a trustworthy vendor.

Don't these cameras help solve crimes?

They can. But evidence gathered through a system with documented security failures can be thrown out in court — as happened in Norfolk, Virginia, where a federal judge ruled ALPR data collection without a warrant constitutes a search under the Fourth Amendment. We're protecting EPPD's ability to make cases stick by ensuring their tools are legally sound.

Can federal immigration agencies access El Paso's Flock data?

El Paso PD says no. But in other cities, federal agencies accessed Flock data for over a year without the police department's knowledge — Flock enabled it without telling them. El Paso is a border city. We deserve a written, enforceable guarantee in the contract — not verbal assurances.

The cameras are grant-funded — doesn't that make them free?

The initial deployment was covered by a state grant. But contracts renew, and future costs fall on El Paso taxpayers. More importantly: the cost of data liability, legal challenges, or a security breach could far exceed the original grant amount.

What do you actually want the city to do?

We're asking the city to cancel the Flock contract and not renew it. If the council refuses, these are the minimum conditions we demand before any renewal is signed — and we will be watching.
