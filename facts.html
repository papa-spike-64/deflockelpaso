Facts & Evidence

What El Paso deserves to know before May 16

Every claim on this page is sourced from government audits, court filings, the federal cybersecurity database, vendor documents, or named investigative reporting. No anonymous sources. No speculation. Click any source link to verify for yourself.

01 — Security

Flock's security record

The National Vulnerability Database is the U.S. government's official registry of cybersecurity flaws. Here is what it says about Flock devices.

CVE Database

22 confirmed security vulnerabilities in Flock devices

The National Vulnerability Database (nvd.nist.gov) lists 22 confirmed CVEs — Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures — specifically affecting Flock Safety hardware. These are not allegations. They are formally registered security flaws in the U.S. government's official cybersecurity database.

Source: nvd.nist.gov — search "Flock Safety"
Physical Access

Cameras can be hacked in 30 seconds via physical button sequence

Security researchers demonstrated on video that Flock cameras can be accessed by anyone who can physically reach them using a simple button sequence. No special equipment required. Many Flock cameras are mounted on public poles at street level.

Source: Documented in security research; cited in U.S. Senator Ron Wyden's investigation into Flock Safety
Password Security

No multi-factor authentication required — passwords were stolen

Senator Ron Wyden's federal investigation found that Flock does not require multi-factor authentication for officer accounts. At least 35 customer account passwords were stolen by hackers. At one point, Flock cameras and admin dashboards could be accessed publicly without any login.

Source: U.S. Senator Ron Wyden investigation, published findings
02 — El Paso Specific

Questions specific to our city

These are issues documented in El Paso's own public records and transparency portal. They deserve direct answers from city leadership before any contract renewal.

⏳ Open Records Request in Progress

A community member has filed a public records request with the City of El Paso seeking detailed data on how El Paso's Flock system has been accessed, by whom, and for what purpose. We are awaiting the city's response. This page will be updated when records are received. Transparency is not a favor — it is a legal right.

El Paso Transparency Portal

Harris County commissioner listed as having data access — no explanation given

El Paso PD's own Flock transparency portal — a public document — lists a Harris County Precinct 3 Commissioner's office as an entity with access to El Paso's camera data. A commissioner's office is not a law enforcement agency. No public explanation has been provided for why a political office in Harris County, hundreds of miles away, has access to El Paso's surveillance data.

We are not asserting wrongdoing. We are asserting that El Paso residents deserve a clear, public answer to a straightforward question: why does this office have access, under what authority, and what data has it accessed?

Source: El Paso PD Flock Transparency Portal (public record) — transparency.flocksafety.com/elpaso-pd; reported by El Paso News
Texas DPS

Texas DPS investigated Flock's required operating license

The Texas Department of Public Safety launched an investigation into whether Flock Safety holds the private security license required to legally operate surveillance systems in Texas. Operating without that license raises questions about the legal standing of El Paso's existing contract and any renewal.

Source: El Paso News, December 2025
Border City Context

El Paso has no state-level data protection for ALPR systems

Texas has no state law governing how license plate reader data must be stored, who can access it, or how long it can be retained. Multiple bills have been introduced in the Texas Legislature — all died in committee. This means El Paso residents' protections exist only in the contract itself — the same contract Flock rewrote with 147 changes in February 2026.

For a border city, this gap is especially significant. When there is no state law and the contract language is controlled by the vendor, the only protection is the vendor's good faith.

Source: Texas Legislature bill tracking; toolkit analysis by DeflockYourCity
Immigration

Federal immigration access: assurances vs. enforceable guarantees

El Paso PD has stated verbally that it does not share data with federal immigration agencies. However, in other cities, Flock enabled federal agency access for extended periods without the local police department's knowledge. The department did not authorize it — the vendor did it anyway.

For El Paso — a majority-Hispanic border city — the difference between a verbal assurance and a written, enforceable contract provision matters enormously. The current contract has not been released publicly in full.

Source: El Paso Matters, March 2026
03 — The Contract

What Flock changed — and what it means

In February 2026, Flock rewrote its Terms of Service. This is the contract framework any renewal would fall under.

Terms of Service

147 changes in Flock's February 2026 Terms rewrite

Flock Safety rewrote its master Terms of Service in February 2026, making 147 documented changes. One of the most significant: the deletion of the clause that previously stated "Flock does not own and shall not sell Customer Data." That language no longer exists in the current terms.

Source: Documented by DeflockYourCity toolkit; independently verifiable via Flock's published terms
Platform Capabilities

Flock's patent describes classifying people by race, gender, height, and weight

Flock Safety holds US Patent 11416545B1, which describes technology capable of classifying individuals by race, gender, height, and weight from camera footage. El Paso's contract covers Flock cameras. Residents should know whether these capabilities are enabled, disabled, or contractually prohibited in El Paso's deployment.

Source: US Patent 11416545B1 (public record, searchable at patents.google.com)
Nova Platform

Flock is building a data platform that tracks individuals without a warrant

In May 2025, investigative outlet 404 Media reported that Flock was developing "Nova" — described internally as a "public safety data platform" that supplements ALPR data with commercial data sources to track specific individuals without a warrant. The Electronic Frontier Foundation called it a "dystopian panopticon." Nova was already in use by law enforcement in an Early Access program before the reporting surfaced.

Source: 404 Media, May 2025; Electronic Frontier Foundation
04 — Vendor Claims vs. Record

What Flock says vs. what the record shows

El Paso city officials have cited Flock's assurances. Here is how those assurances compare to documented incidents in other cities.

The claim What the record shows Status
"Data will not be shared with federal agencies" In multiple cities, Flock enabled federal access without the police department's knowledge or authorization. The department found out after the fact. ⚠ Disputed by record
"Cameras only capture the back of vehicles and license plates" Flock's own patent (US11416545B1) describes classifying individuals by race, gender, height, and weight. Flock's "Condor" PTZ cameras can zoom in on faces. The platform's capabilities exceed what officers are told it does. ⚠ Incomplete
"We have 30-day data retention" 30-day retention is a dashboard setting — not a contractual guarantee. The contract language and the dashboard setting are two different things. Settings can be changed by the vendor. ⚠ Needs verification
"Multi-factor authentication protects the system" El Paso PD states MFA is required. Flock previously did not require it system-wide. Independent verification of El Paso's specific configuration has not been made public. ⏳ Unverified
"Cambridge, MA terminated for breach of trust" After Cambridge City Council voted to deactivate the cameras, Flock installed additional cameras anyway without authorization. The contract was terminated for "breach of trust." ✓ Confirmed
05 — Our Position

What we're asking El Paso City Council to do

We are calling for the contract to be canceled and not renewed. If the city insists on renewing despite public opposition, these are the minimum conditions we demand — and we will be watching.

  • 01 Publish the full current contract publicly before any renewal is signed
  • 02 Provide a public explanation for the Harris County commissioner data access listed on EPPD's transparency portal
  • 03 Require an independent security audit — not a vendor self-assessment
  • 04 Cap data retention by city ordinance, not just by a vendor dashboard setting
  • 05 Require a written, enforceable prohibition on federal immigration agency access — not a verbal assurance
  • 06 Require a council vote before any new data-sharing agreements are activated
  • 07 Clarify the status of the Texas DPS licensing investigation before renewal
  • 08 Confirm in writing which Flock platform capabilities are active, disabled, or contractually prohibited in El Paso's deployment
