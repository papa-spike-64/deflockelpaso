The Flock contract was renewed. Here is what happened and what comes next.

April 28, 2026 Contract Renewal DeFlock El Paso

On April 28, 2026, the City of El Paso renewed its contract with Flock Safety. It happened through an administrative Motor Vehicle Crime Prevention Authority (MVCPA) grant application — not through a second full council vote specifically on whether to renew. The contract that community members, council members, and this organization spent months raising alarms about is now extended.

We are not going to pretend that isn't a setback. It is. The city renewed a contract with a vendor that had quietly removed its data-sale prohibition two months earlier, that runs permanently unpatched security software, and whose audit logs go to ICE rather than to the city that contracted with it. None of that changed before the renewal was signed.

But here is what else is true: an amendment passed that requires EPPD to write future MVCPA grant applications without naming Flock Safety exclusively. That is a real accountability opening — and it only matters if people keep pushing.

Before we get to April 28: EPPD said the contract didn't exist

In late March 2026, a community member submitted a Texas Public Information Act request to El Paso PD — Open Records Request #W200992-032526 — asking for "Contract between the City of El Paso and Flock Group Inc., doing business under the name Flock Safety."

EPPD's response, from Police Records Specialist Antonio Flores: "Please be advised that I have been informed that there are no responsive documents pertaining to your request. As no responsive documents have been located, there is no charge associated with this response and your request is now considered closed."

The contract was produced approximately two hours before the April 28 council vote. EPPD attributed the failure to a clerical error.

A community member asked for the contract that governs how 150 surveillance cameras photograph every vehicle in El Paso. The police department responsible for those cameras said the contract didn't exist. It was produced two hours before the council voted on funding it for another year. This is the transparency record of the city's Flock Safety program.

The numbers

April 28, 2026 — MVCPA Grant Renewal
MVCPA grant funds $693,334
El Paso tax dollars $138,666
Total renewal cost $832,000

For context: the original deployment was funded by a $702,500 state grant for 150 cameras — the hardware. This renewal adds $138,666 in El Paso tax dollars on top of the new grant funds. The cameras were never free. They were never even just "grant-funded." And now El Paso residents are directly on the hook for a portion of the ongoing cost.

What the April 28 vote actually was — and wasn't

The April 28 council meeting addressed agenda item 23: the MVCPA grant application to fund Flock Safety for another year. This was not a standalone vote on whether to continue the Flock contract. The underlying contract contains an auto-renewal clause (Section 7.1) requiring written notice of non-renewal at least 30 days before the term ends — otherwise it renews automatically. Whether the contract had already auto-renewed before the April 28 meeting was raised during public comment and was never addressed in council discussion.

What passed on April 28 was the grant application and an amendment requiring EPPD to write future grant applications without naming Flock exclusively. The cameras keep running. The contract keeps running. The accountability questions remain open.

The amendment matters — but only if it's enforced

The vendor-selection amendment is the most significant accountability development to come out of this process. EPPD must write the next MVCPA grant application without naming Flock Safety exclusively. In practice, that means El Paso can now choose from any vendor on the state's Buy Board — not just Flock.

EPPD told council that if acceptable terms cannot be negotiated with Flock, a different vendor will be chosen.

This is an opening, not a guarantee. The amendment does not require EPPD to choose a different vendor. It does not require a competitive bidding process. It does not mandate locally governed alternatives. What it does is remove the automatic assumption that Flock gets renewed again next time — if residents, council members, and advocates hold EPPD to the language.

Whether that lever gets used depends on what happens between now and the next grant application. Reps. Canales and Limón have been consistent advocates throughout this process. The six members who voted against the Canales-Limón cancellation proposal in March are now on record in a different context — one where "let's learn more" has no cover left. The information is public. The contract is on file. The amendment is in writing.

What changed — and what didn't

✓ Amendment requires open vendor selection — EPPD must write the next grant without naming Flock exclusively. A path to a different vendor now exists on paper.
✓ The contract is now on file — We have Flock_Safety_Contract_-_COEP_2025.pdf and the February 2026 updated Terms and Conditions. El Paso residents can read what was signed.
✓ This is now part of the public record — Two council members put accountability on the agenda in March. The vendor's documented failures are part of El Paso's public council record in a way they weren't before.
✗ Android 8.1 vulnerabilities not fixed — The cameras still run a permanently unpatched operating system with 22 confirmed federal security vulnerabilities.
✗ Harris County data access unexplained — EPPD's own transparency portal still lists a Harris County commissioner's office with data access. No public explanation has been given.
✗ Data-sale prohibition still deleted — The February 2026 Terms rewrite stands. The renewal was signed under terms that no longer prohibit Flock from selling customer data.
✗ No written ICE access prohibition — El Paso still has no written, enforceable prohibition on federal immigration agency access. In a border city with a large immigrant community, a verbal assurance is not sufficient.
✗ Texas DPS investigation status unresolved — The investigation into whether Flock holds the private security license required to operate in Texas has not been publicly resolved.

Our view

The renewal is a failure of the process that should have happened before 150 cameras were quietly deployed across El Paso — and a second failure of the process that should have happened before the renewal was signed. A city council that debated this contract publicly for the first time in March, two weeks before the renewal deadline, did not have the conditions in place to make a fully informed decision. The administrative pathway used on April 28 closed off a second council vote on the core question.

"The cameras are still running. The accountability questions are still open. The fight continues."

We also want to be honest about what the amendment represents. Getting EPPD on record as required to consider alternatives — not just automatically re-up with Flock — is something. It is not what this community deserved. But it is a foothold, and footholds matter when the alternative is nothing.

Reps. Canales and Limón did not get the outcome they pushed for on March 3. What they got instead is a record — of what the vendor's failures are, of which council members were on which side, and of what conditions were never met before this renewal was signed. That record will matter when the next grant application comes up.

What you can do now

The most useful thing you can do right now is contact your council member and put the accountability conditions on the record — specifically, demand enforcement of the vendor-selection amendment and a public accounting of the questions that were never answered before April 28. The Take Action page has updated scripts for the post-renewal situation.

We are also asking anyone who attends council meetings to show up and speak during public comment when surveillance technology or the MVCPA grant comes up again. The timeline for the next grant application is not yet public — but it will come.

We have the contract on file. We will publish our analysis of it. If you have received responses to public records requests related to the Flock contract — search logs, communications with federal agencies, data-sharing agreements — we want to know. Contact us at contact@deflockelpaso.org.
